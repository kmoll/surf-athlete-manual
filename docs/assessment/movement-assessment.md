## Purpose

Check whether the surfer has controlled access to the positions surfing and its
supporting training actually require, before adding load or volume to those
positions.

This is a screening method, not a diagnostic test and not a competition. It
identifies where to spend limited mobility and motor-control time.

## Why It Matters for Surfing

Paddling requires sustained shoulder flexion and thoracic extension. The pop-up
requires hip flexion, ankle dorsiflexion, and rotation under time pressure.
Bottom turns and cutbacks require controlled hip and trunk rotation. Loading a
restricted or poorly controlled position tends to produce compensation,
inefficient technique, or injury risk rather than useful adaptation.

## Source

The principle of screening fundamental movement before loading it follows Gray
Cook's general teaching that movement quality should be assessed before
capacity is trained. Kelly Starrett's general principle of testing the specific
positions a person actually needs, rather than only general flexibility,
informs the position list below. This page does not reproduce a proprietary
or trademarked screening battery; the checks below are general exercise
physiology applied to the demands of surfing.

## Principles

- Screen the positions surfing and training actually use, not every possible
  joint range.
- Compare each check against the surfer's own prior result, not a universal
  numeric standard.
- A restriction is information, not a failure.
- Reassess the same way each time so results are comparable.
- Movement quality work should follow directly from what the assessment finds.

## Protocol

Perform each check in a warmed-up state, without forcing end range. For each
position, record one of three qualitative results in the private workspace:
**clear** (full, controlled access), **limited** (accessible with noticeable
restriction, asymmetry, or compensation), or **restricted** (position is not
accessible without significant compensation or discomfort).

1. **Prone paddle position.** From lying face down, prop up onto the forearms
   or hands as in a paddling posture. Note shoulder, thoracic, and low-back
   comfort and how long the position can be held with good form.
2. **Overhead shoulder reach.** Raise both arms overhead against a wall or
   floor, checking for symmetry and whether the low back arches to compensate.
3. **Ankle dorsiflexion.** Check how far the knee can travel over the toes
   without the heel lifting, on each side, related to Ben Patrick's emphasis on
   knee-over-toe ankle and knee capacity for athletic positions.
4. **Hip hinge and squat depth.** Check whether a controlled hip hinge and a
   bodyweight squat to a comfortable depth can be performed with a neutral
   spine, consistent with Stuart McGill's spine-sparing movement principles.
5. **Seated or standing trunk rotation.** Check active rotation range and
   control to each side, noting any asymmetry relevant to the pop-up and
   turning.
6. **Single-leg stance.** Check balance and control on each leg for a
   consistent duration, noting asymmetry.

## Common Mistakes

- Forcing end-range positions to "pass" a check.
- Testing only once and never reassessing.
- Treating a restricted result as an emergency rather than a training priority.
- Screening general flexibility instead of the positions surfing actually uses.
- Skipping the check on a symptomatic or painful side instead of noting it.

## Reassessment

Reassess on a schedule that fits the training block, such as at the start of
each block and after a significant break from training or surfing. Track
results as trends in the private workspace rather than as a public benchmark.
A limited or restricted result that does not improve with focused daily
movement work over several weeks is a signal to seek qualified evaluation
rather than to continue self-managing it.
