## Purpose

Track paddle capacity — the broad ability to sustain and repeat paddling —
separately from paddle volume, the measured amount of paddling performed.

This page defines the method only. Actual durations, counts, and results
belong in the private workspace, not in the public manual.

## Why It Matters for Surfing

Paddling is the primary sport-specific conditioning method described in
[Decision 0002: Paddling Is Primary](../decisions/0002-paddling-primary-conditioning.md).
Getting into position, chasing sets, and paddling back out repeatedly across a
session depends on capacity that is specific to the prone paddle position and
is not fully captured by general aerobic conditioning.

## Source

The methods on this page are labeled **general exercise physiology**, applying
sport-specific field testing principles to paddling. They are not attributed to
an approved individual expert.

## Principles

- Assess paddling in the prone paddle position, not a generic proxy movement.
- Track technique quality under fatigue, not only speed or distance.
- Track repeatability across efforts, consistent with the intermittent demand
  of a surf session.
- Use equipment and conditions that can be reasonably reproduced.
- Water-based assessment inherits ocean and open-water risk; apply the same
  safety judgment used for any water session.

## Protocol

### 1. Technique-Under-Fatigue Check

Paddle at a strong, sustainable effort for a fixed, familiar duration or
distance. Note where in the effort technique first begins to degrade, such as
a shortened stroke, increased head lift, or loss of a stable line. An
improving trend is technique holding for longer before it degrades, not just a
faster time.

### 2. Repeat-Effort Check

Perform a set of short, hard paddle efforts with a fixed active-recovery
interval between them, similar in structure to the
[Norwegian 4x4 approach](../conditioning/index.md#norwegian-4x4-intervals)
applied to paddling. Note pace or stroke count and perceived effort for each
effort. A smaller decline across repeated efforts indicates better
repeatability. Treat this as a hard session for the week.

### 3. Sustained Cruising Check

Paddle at an easy, conversational effort for a fixed familiar duration or
distance to check general paddle endurance and comfort in the position,
separate from hard-effort capacity.

## Common Mistakes

- Testing paddle capacity in open water without normal water-safety judgment,
  a buddy, or appropriate conditions.
- Treating a single fast effort as capacity rather than checking repeatability.
- Comparing results to another person's pace or stroke count.
- Running this assessment on top of an already-high surf and paddle week.
- Ignoring the safety gate in the
  [recovery-readiness guidance](../recovery/readiness-guidance.md) to complete
  a scheduled check.

## Reassessment

Reassess at block boundaries or after a significant change in surf or paddle
volume, no more often than every few weeks, using the same duration, distance,
and, where practical, equipment and conditions each time.
