# Conditioning System

## Purpose

Develop the aerobic capacity, high-intensity aerobic power, and repeatability
needed to paddle, surf, and recover across efforts and sessions.

## Why It Matters for Surfing

Surfing alternates lower-intensity movement and waiting with demanding paddle
efforts. Conditioning should improve that capacity without consuming the
recovery needed for paddling, surfing, strength, and adaptation.

## Source

The conditioning methods on this page are labeled **general exercise
physiology**. They are not attributed to an approved individual expert unless a
later protocol provides a supportable citation.

## Priorities

1. Paddling is the primary sport-specific conditioning method.
2. Easy aerobic work supports recovery and aerobic volume.
3. High-intensity aerobic intervals are a tool for VO2 max development.
4. Breath-hold or CO2-tolerance work is optional, low priority, and governed by
   strict safety constraints.

Conditioning volume is not added automatically. The dose must fit around surf
and paddle volume, and recovery has authority over the planned session.

## Norwegian 4x4 Intervals

Norwegian 4x4 intervals are a general exercise physiology tool for developing
VO2 max. The standard structure is four four-minute high-aerobic work intervals
separated by three minutes of active recovery. The original research protocol
targeted 90-95% of maximum heart rate during work intervals and approximately
70% during active recovery.

For this manual:

- Treat the protocol as a hard conditioning session, not a test or a requirement.
- Use a modality that permits sound mechanics and does not add avoidable joint or
  muscle damage.
- Build into the target effort rather than sprinting at the start.
- Finish with technically repeatable work rather than training to failure.
- Reduce, replace, or omit the session when surf volume, paddle volume, sleep,
  soreness, illness, or accumulated fatigue limits recovery.
- Do not copy the research frequency into a program without considering the
  surfer's total weekly stress.

The method can improve VO2 max, but the available research does not establish
one universally optimal frequency or prove direct surf-performance transfer.

## Conservative CO2-Tolerance Guidance

CO2-tolerance and breath-hold work is not a foundational conditioning method in
this manual. Potential performance benefits are limited and uncertain, while
breath-hold blackout can occur without warning and can be fatal in water.

The public manual therefore does not prescribe underwater breath-hold sets,
maximum breath holds, breath-hold challenges, or hyperventilation. Breath-hold
practice must never be performed alone or used to suppress the urge to breathe.
Recreational surfers and older athletes should omit deliberate breath-hold
training. Any breath-hold work considered for high-performance sport requires
appropriate medical screening, qualified direct supervision, a documented
aquatic-safety protocol, and a clear reason that justifies the risk.

When those safeguards are unavailable, omit CO2-tolerance training. Ordinary
breathing practice may be used for relaxation or recovery, but it should not be
presented as a substitute for supervised breath-hold training or as a proven way
to improve surf performance.

Stop immediately for dizziness, confusion, visual changes, chest symptoms,
unusual shortness of breath, loss of motor control, or any urge to continue for
the sake of a personal record.

## Monitoring and Adjustment

- Protect recoverable surf and paddle volume first.
- Count Norwegian 4x4 work as a hard session when planning the week.
- Avoid stacking hard intervals with other high-fatigue sessions unless readiness
  and the purpose of the block justify it.
- Regress to easy aerobic work or rest when readiness is reduced.
- Preserve capacity for an additional hard session at the end of most weeks.
