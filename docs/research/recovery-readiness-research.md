## Purpose

This note reviews evidence that can inform a recovery-readiness decision
framework for the public manual. It is research input, not the finished
framework, a medical assessment, or a diagnosis. See the
[Health and Safety Disclaimer](../appendices/health-safety-disclaimer.md).

## Status

The findings in this note have been synthesized into the public
[Recovery-Readiness Guidance](../recovery/readiness-guidance.md). This note
retains the evidence boundary, source detail, and uncertainty behind that
guidance.

## Why It Matters for Surfing

Surfing, paddling, strength work, conditioning, travel, work, and ordinary life
all draw on the same recovery capacity. A useful readiness process should help a
surfer choose an appropriate training stress without treating normal fatigue as
a disorder or letting one device score make the decision.

## Source Boundary

The approved expert list remains unchanged. Findings from researchers outside
that list are labeled **general exercise physiology**. Their inclusion here does
not make them approved coaching sources or permit unsupported attribution.

## Findings from Approved Sources

### Gray Cook

Cook's Functional Movement Systems guidance supports using pain as a decision
boundary: pain during a screen ends that screen and calls for appropriate
evaluation. He has also proposed that a change from an established movement
baseline may provide useful recovery information, while explicitly recognizing
that this idea is not a validated standalone recovery measure.

Application: pain should change the decision path. Movement quality may provide
context when compared with the athlete's normal baseline, but it should not be
converted into a universal readiness score.

### Mark Rippetoe

Rippetoe describes training as a stress-recovery-adaptation process. The useful
training stress must be large enough to cause adaptation and small enough to be
recovered from. His recovery discussion emphasizes adequate food and sleep and
recognizes that recoverable training volume and frequency change with the
athlete and over time.

Application: the daily decision is not whether the athlete can tolerate any
work. It is what dose can be recovered from while preserving the intended
adaptation.

### Limitation of the Approved Source Set

The reviewed approved-source material supports recovery-first programming,
pain-related stop rules, technical quality, and recoverable training doses. It
does not provide one validated daily readiness algorithm or universal set of
cutoffs. The public framework should not invent one or attribute one to these
experts.

## Findings from Recovery Research

The following contributors are included as **general exercise physiology**, not
as additions to the approved expert list.

- Michael Kellmann and colleagues frame recovery and performance as a balance
  between total stress and recovery, with recovery planned for the individual.
- Shona Halson's monitoring review finds no single definitive fatigue marker and
  recommends practical, individualized monitoring interpreted in context.
- Anna Saw, Luana Main, and Paul Gastin report that subjective self-report
  measures often respond more consistently to training load than commonly used
  objective measures.
- Romain Meeusen and colleagues distinguish short, productive fatigue from
  prolonged maladaptation and stress that require a different response.
- Neil Walsh and colleagues identify sleep as a central athlete-health and
  recovery behavior.
- Inigo Mujika's taper research supports reducing training load to dissipate
  accumulated fatigue while retaining fitness before important performance.
- Olivier Dupuy, Laurent Bosquet, and colleagues find that recovery modalities
  can affect soreness and perceived fatigue, but results vary by method and
  outcome.

## Evidence-Informed Principles

### No Single Marker Decides Readiness

Readiness should be a short decision process, not a verdict from one number.
Sleep, perceived fatigue, soreness, stress, mood or motivation, recent workload,
pain or illness, and a familiar submaximal warm-up can each contribute useful
context.

Resting heart rate or heart-rate variability may add information when measured
consistently and interpreted as a personal trend. Current evidence does not
justify letting one daily wearable score automatically prescribe training.

### Subjective Information Is Real Information

A brief, consistently worded check-in can be useful. Relevant domains include:

- sleep quality and duration
- general fatigue
- muscle soreness
- life stress
- mood and motivation
- perceived recovery

The purpose is to notice meaningful changes from the athlete's normal pattern,
not to reward a high score or diagnose a condition.

### Total Stress Matters

The decision should consider recent surfing, paddling, strength work,
conditioning, travel, and life demands together. Training load alone does not
describe the recovery problem.

### Trends Matter More Than Isolated Bad Days

One poor night or a single difficult warm-up may justify a smaller session.
Repeated decline, persistent pain, illness, unusual symptoms, or performance
loss despite reduced training requires a more conservative response and may
require qualified medical assessment. The manual should not diagnose
overtraining syndrome.

## Recovery Options

### Tier 1: Foundational Actions

These options address the recovery constraint directly and should come before
specialized modalities:

- reduce, replace, or postpone the next training stress
- protect a consistent sleep opportunity and address obvious sleep disruption
- eat normal meals that provide adequate energy, carbohydrate, protein, and
  fluids for the work performed
- use rest, relaxed walking, or easy movement when it improves how the athlete
  feels without becoming another workout
- address the life-stress or scheduling problem when it is the main constraint

These are general exercise physiology principles. Nutrition or hydration needs
that involve a medical condition require individualized professional advice.

### Tier 2: Optional Symptom-Management Tools

Massage, compression garments, cold-water immersion, contrast water, and other
modalities may reduce soreness or perceived fatigue in some contexts. They are
optional tools for a specific short-term outcome, not proof that adaptation has
accelerated and not substitutes for sleep, adequate food, or load adjustment.

Cold-water immersion should not be automatic after every strength session. Its
use depends on the immediate priority, because evidence about recovery of
symptoms and evidence about long-term training adaptation answer different
questions.

### Match the Option to the Constraint

| Primary constraint | First response | Possible addition | Important limit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Short or disrupted sleep | Reduce the planned stress and protect the next sleep opportunity | A nap when practical | A modality does not repay persistent sleep loss |
| Inadequate food or fluids for the work performed | Restore normal meals and hydration | Adjust meal timing around training | Supplements do not replace adequate intake |
| High general fatigue or life stress | Reduce, replace, or postpone training | Relaxed walking or easy movement | Active recovery should not become another hard session |
| Expected muscle soreness | Time and an appropriate next-session dose | Massage, compression, or water immersion if useful | Less soreness does not necessarily mean faster adaptation |
| Short turnaround after hard conditioning or competition | Protect sleep, food, hydration, and reduce avoidable load | Cold-water immersion may support selected short-term outcomes | Do not make it automatic after strength training |
| Accumulated fatigue before an important surf opportunity | Reduce training load while retaining some intensity and skill exposure | Use a planned taper | Do not add last-minute fitness work that cannot be recovered from |
| Pain, illness, injury concern, or unusual symptoms | Apply the safety gate and seek appropriate evaluation when indicated | None until the concern is understood | Do not use recovery tools to conceal a warning sign |

## Readiness Inputs Used by the Guidance

The public guidance uses the smallest practical set of inputs supported by this
review:

1. **Safety gate:** pain, illness, injury concern, or unusual symptoms.
2. **Recovery trend:** sleep, fatigue, soreness, stress, mood, and motivation
   compared with the athlete's normal range.
3. **Recent load:** surf, paddle, strength, conditioning, travel, and life load.
4. **Performance confirmation:** a familiar, low-risk warm-up or submaximal task.
5. **Optional objective context:** consistently collected resting heart rate or
   heart-rate variability trend.

## Research-to-Guidance Decision Structure

The public guidance keeps the decision process simple:

1. Apply the safety gate.
2. Look for a meaningful change or multi-day trend, not perfection.
3. Compare the planned stress with recent total load.
4. Choose to continue, reduce, replace, or rest.
5. Confirm the choice during the warm-up and change it if movement quality,
   symptoms, or performance disagree.
6. Record the decision and outcome so the athlete's own baseline improves the
   process over time.

## Uncertainty and Research Gaps

- There is no universal readiness score or cutoff that is valid for every
  athlete and training context.
- Self-report is useful but influenced by expectations and inconsistent wording.
- Consumer devices and heart-rate variability methods differ.
- Many modality studies measure soreness or short-term performance rather than
  long-term adaptation.
- Surf-specific readiness research is limited, so applications to surfing are
  reasoned extensions of broader sport evidence.
- The guidance derived from this note needs conservative rules, clear scope, and
  revision as it is used and reviewed.
