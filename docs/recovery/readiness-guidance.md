## Purpose

Use current recovery information to choose an appropriate training action:
**proceed**, **modify**, **recover**, or **evaluate**.

This guidance does not produce a readiness score. It supports a practical
decision using the surfer's normal baseline, recent total load, and response to
a warm-up.

## Why It Matters for Surfing

Surfing, paddling, strength, conditioning, travel, and life stress all draw on
the same recovery capacity. Following the schedule without considering that
total demand can replace adaptation with accumulated fatigue.

The goal is not to feel perfect before every session. The goal is to apply a
training stress that can be recovered from while preserving movement quality
and the next important surf opportunity.

## Source

This guidance applies the accepted [recovery-first decision](../decisions/0001-recovery-first.md)
and the evidence summarized in the

Research outside the approved expert list is labeled general exercise

## Principles

- Recovery governs planned volume and intensity.
- Pain, illness, injury concerns, and unusual symptoms are safety questions, not
  low-readiness scores.
- Compare current signals with the surfer's own normal pattern.
- Consider training, surfing, travel, and life stress together.
- Use the warm-up to confirm or change the initial decision.
- Prefer the smallest change that restores a recoverable training dose.
- Record useful decisions and outcomes without turning recovery into another
  performance contest.

## Guidance

### 1. Apply the Safety Gate

Choose **evaluate** rather than continuing as planned when there is:

- pain that changes movement or is new, severe, worsening, or unusual
- a suspected injury
- illness or symptoms made worse by training
- dizziness, faintness, chest discomfort, unusual breathing difficulty, or
  another concerning symptom
- a persistent decline that has not improved after training was reduced

Stop or modify the activity and seek appropriately qualified evaluation when
indicated. Use local emergency services for a medical emergency. Do not use a
recovery method to conceal a warning sign. See the
[Health and Safety Disclaimer](../appendices/health-safety-disclaimer.md).

### 2. Check the Current Pattern

Briefly consider:

- sleep quality and duration
- general fatigue
- muscle soreness
- life stress
- mood and motivation
- recent surf and paddle volume
- recent strength and conditioning load
- travel, schedule disruption, heat, and other environmental demand

The question is whether something is meaningfully different from normal, not
whether every signal is ideal. Do not add the answers into a composite score.
Resting heart rate or heart-rate variability may provide optional context when
collected consistently, but a wearable does not make the decision.

### 3. Choose the Initial Action

#### Proceed

Proceed with the planned session when:

- the safety gate is clear
- recovery signals are broadly consistent with the normal pattern
- recent total load fits the planned training stress
- the athlete expects to maintain the required movement and technical quality

Proceed does not require feeling fully rested or free from ordinary soreness.

#### Modify

Modify the session when the safety gate is clear but recovery is meaningfully
below normal, recent total load is high, or the planned session is unlikely to
be completed with good quality.

Use the smallest effective adjustment:

1. Remove optional work.
2. Reduce total sets, repetitions, intervals, or session duration.
3. Keep useful practice but lower the effort or loading.
4. Replace hard conditioning with easy paddling, walking, mobility, or technique
   work when that is genuinely restorative.
5. Postpone the session when a smaller change will not make it recoverable.

Do not compensate for the reduction by adding unrelated work.

#### Recover

Choose recovery instead of training when there is no immediate safety concern,
but accumulated fatigue, poor sleep, high life stress, or a failed warm-up makes
useful training unlikely.

Prioritize:

- reducing or postponing training stress
- protecting the next sleep opportunity
- normal meals and hydration appropriate to the work performed
- rest or genuinely easy movement
- resolving the schedule or life-stress constraint where possible

Massage, compression, and water-based modalities are optional tools for
short-term soreness or perceived recovery. They do not replace load adjustment,
sleep, food, or qualified care.

#### Evaluate

Choose evaluation when the safety gate identifies a concern or when symptoms or
performance continue to worsen despite reduced training. Evaluation means
getting the right help for the concern; it is not a diagnosis made by this
manual or by an AI assistant.

### 4. Confirm During the Warm-Up

Use a familiar, low-risk warm-up as the final check.

- If movement, symptoms, and performance are normal for the day, continue with
  the initial decision.
- If quality improves but remains below normal, keep the session modified.
- If quality deteriorates, symptoms appear, or ordinary work feels unexpectedly
  difficult, move from proceed to modify, recover, or evaluate.

The schedule does not overrule the warm-up.

### 5. Record Only What Improves the Next Decision

Keep personal records in the private workspace. A useful entry contains:

- what was planned
- the chosen action
- the signals that materially affected the decision
- what was changed
- how the warm-up affected the decision
- the later or next-day response

Do not publish personal health information, measurements, schedules, or logs in
the public manual.

## Common Mistakes

- Waiting to feel perfect before doing any training.
- Treating normal soreness as an injury or ignoring concerning pain as soreness.
- Allowing one bad night, one wearable score, or one good mood to decide alone.
- Evaluating only gym work while ignoring surfing, paddling, travel, and life
  stress.
- Turning an easy recovery session into additional conditioning.
- Completing poor-quality volume because it was written in the plan.
- Using a modality to feel less sore while leaving the actual load problem
  unchanged.

## Monitoring and Adjustment

Review decisions and outcomes over several weeks. Look for recurring situations
in which the same planned work is repeatedly modified or recovery takes longer
than expected. Adjust the program, schedule, or recovery practices rather than
continually overriding the same signal.

The guidance should become more individualized through consistent observation,
not through unsupported universal thresholds.
