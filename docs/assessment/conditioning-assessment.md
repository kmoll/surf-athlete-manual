## Purpose

Track aerobic capacity and repeatability using submaximal field methods that
do not require a maximal-effort test.

This page defines the method only. Actual paces, heart rates, and results
belong in the private workspace, not in the public manual.

## Why It Matters for Surfing

Surfing is intermittent: moderate paddling and waiting interspersed with hard
efforts, repeated across a session. Aerobic conditioning supports the ability
to sustain that pattern and recover between efforts. A maximal aerobic test
adds fatigue and injury risk without providing information that a well-chosen
submaximal method cannot also provide.

## Source

The methods on this page are labeled **general exercise physiology**. They are
not attributed to an approved individual expert. The Norwegian 4x4 interval
structure referenced below follows the same source described on the
[Conditioning system](../conditioning/index.md) page.

## Principles

- Prefer submaximal, repeatable field checks over maximal-effort testing.
- Track trends in the surfer's own pace, heart rate, and perceived effort over
  time rather than comparing to a universal standard.
- Use conditions that can be reasonably reproduced, such as similar terrain,
  water conditions, or equipment.
- Treat repeatability across efforts, not just single-effort output, as the
  primary signal.

## Protocol

### 1. Easy-Effort Trend Check

Perform a familiar easy-effort aerobic session, such as easy paddling, cycling,
or running, at a consistent perceived effort using the talk test: an effort at
which full sentences can still be spoken comfortably. Record pace or output,
heart rate if available, and perceived effort. Repeated over time at the same
perceived effort, an improving pace or lower heart rate indicates improving
aerobic capacity.

### 2. Interval Repeatability Check

Using the [Norwegian 4x4 structure](../conditioning/index.md#norwegian-4x4-intervals)
at a fixed, sustainable target effort, record output or heart rate for each of
the four work intervals. A smaller decline in output or a smaller heart-rate
drift across the four intervals indicates better repeatability. This check
should be treated as a hard conditioning session for the week, not an
additional one.

### 3. Recovery Trend Check

After a hard aerobic effort, note the general trend of how quickly heart rate
and perceived effort return toward baseline during easy movement or rest.
Track the trend over weeks rather than treating any single session as
diagnostic.

## Common Mistakes

- Performing a maximal-effort test to establish a baseline.
- Comparing results to another person's pace or heart-rate numbers.
- Changing terrain, equipment, or conditions between checks and treating the
  result as a clean comparison.
- Running the interval repeatability check on a week that is already carrying
  a high surf, paddle, or strength load.
- Ignoring the safety gate in the
  [recovery-readiness guidance](../recovery/readiness-guidance.md) to complete
  a scheduled check.

## Reassessment

Reassess the easy-effort trend informally over ongoing training and the
interval repeatability check at block boundaries, no more often than every few
weeks, to avoid turning conditioning assessment into additional hard-session
volume.
