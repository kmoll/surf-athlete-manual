## Purpose

Establish a reusable method for assessing strength that prioritizes technical
proficiency and relative strength over maximal testing, consistent with
[Decision 0003: Relative Strength Over Size](../decisions/0003-relative-strength-over-size.md).

This page defines the method only. Actual loads, results, and trends belong in
the private workspace, not in the public manual.

## Why It Matters for Surfing

Surf performance depends on strength relative to body mass that can be
expressed through sound movement patterns, not on maximal loads lifted in
isolation. A strength assessment that risks injury or requires extended
recovery works against the same capacity it is meant to protect.

## Source

Technical standards for the squat, hinge, press, and pull patterns follow Mark
Rippetoe's coaching cues for barbell technique. The preference for
submaximal-effort testing over frequent maximal testing follows Pavel
Tsatsouline's general principle of training the nervous system without
grinding, and leaving repetitions in reserve rather than testing to failure.
Hinge and loaded-carry technique screening follows Stuart McGill's
spine-sparing movement principles.

## Principles

- Confirm technique before assessing load.
- Use submaximal effort with repetitions in reserve rather than a true one-rep
  maximum.
- Assess patterns relevant to surfing: squat, hinge, press, pull, carry, and
  rotational or anti-rotational control.
- Compare results to the surfer's own prior assessment, expressed relative to
  body mass, not to a public numeric standard.
- Reassess infrequently enough to avoid turning training sessions into repeated
  maximal tests.

## Protocol

### 1. Technical Proficiency Check

For each pattern below, confirm the surfer can perform several controlled
repetitions at a light-to-moderate load with the technical standard met on
every repetition. Do not proceed to a loaded strength check for a pattern that
does not yet meet its technical standard; continue technique practice instead.

- **Squat pattern:** consistent depth, neutral spine, and knee tracking.
- **Hinge pattern:** neutral spine throughout, hip-dominant movement, no lumbar
  flexion under load.
- **Press pattern:** stable trunk and shoulder position without excessive
  lumbar arch.
- **Pull pattern:** controlled shoulder-blade and trunk position without
  momentum substituting for strength.
- **Loaded carry:** upright posture and even gait under load.

### 2. Submaximal Strength Check

Once technique is confirmed, use a working set of approximately five
repetitions performed with at least two repetitions left in reserve as the
reference effort for each pattern. Record the load relative to body mass in
the private workspace. This is a reference point for tracking trends, not a
maximal test, and it should not be pursued to technical failure.

### 3. Rotational and Anti-Rotational Control

Check the surfer's ability to resist or produce controlled trunk rotation
under load, such as a controlled anti-rotation hold or a light rotational
carry, noting asymmetry between sides.

## Common Mistakes

- Testing a true one-rep maximum as a routine assessment.
- Assessing load before technique is consistent.
- Comparing results against another person's numbers instead of the surfer's
  own trend relative to body mass.
- Reassessing so frequently that assessment itself becomes a fatiguing session.
- Ignoring an asymmetry found during the rotational check.

## Reassessment

Reassess at block boundaries rather than every session. A single submaximal
check per pattern per assessment window is sufficient to track a trend without
adding meaningful fatigue.
